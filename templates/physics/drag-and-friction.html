Drag

While not fundamental laws of nature, drag and friction are constently in effect in life on Earth. Drag is the force of resistance against movement from a fluid, such as air or water, when something is moving through it. So for example if someone throws a ball through the air, the ball is constantly having to push air particles out of the way, which slows the ball down. This, together with gravity, is the reason that when we throw a ball, it doesn't just keep going out the same speed in a straight line. It is being acted upon by drag and gravity and so these forces are changing the ball's path.

We can see how big an effect drag has when looking at the fastest speeds by vehicles set on Earth vs. in space:

  • The fastest speed ever recorded by a vehicle on earth was by the x-15 rocketship in 1967 going at a speed of 7,232 Km/hr.
  • The fastest spacecraft Helios 2 however set a record in 1976 of a whopping 252,792 Km/hr.

Why such a big difference?

The largest factor is drag. On earth an areoplane is constantly being slowed down by the air. It's always bumping into air particles that take energy away from the plane, so the plane has to always be burning fuel to stay at the same speed. In fact, all aircraft will reach a speed where the acceleration from the engine is the same as the decceleration from the drag and so it can go no faster. On the other hand a rocket travelling through space is in a vacuum, meaning there is no air to slow the rocket down. If the rocket keeps accelerating it will just get faster and faster.

Friction

Friction is a similar to drag in that it is a force of resistance against movement, but it is between solids. An example would be if you press your thumb hard against the table and then try to move it to the side, you'll notice that at first, when you put just a small amount of force in, your thumb won't move. You have to put more force in to move it. This is because friction is resisting the movement, and so you must put in enough force to overcome the friction.

Now the magnitude of the friction is down to the surfaces that are coming into contact. For instance ice produces very little friction where as concrete would produce much more. We could observe this by rolling a tennis ball gently along some ice or concrete. With the same initial force, the tennis ball will go much further on the ice because there is much less energy being taken out by the ice.

Now what do we mean when we say energy being "taken out of" the ice? Where does it go? Is it destroyed?
